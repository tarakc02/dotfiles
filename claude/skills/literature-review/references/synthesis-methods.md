# Literature Synthesis Methods

This reference covers techniques for moving from a collection of individual sources to a coherent, analytical synthesis.

## The Fundamental Distinction: Summary vs. Synthesis

- **Summary**: Recaps what individual sources say, one by one
- **Synthesis**: Reorganizes and connects information across sources to build new understanding

A literature review must synthesize. If each paragraph discusses a single source, you are summarizing, not synthesizing.

### Example: Summary (poor)
> Smith (2021) found that X. Jones (2022) studied Y and found Z. Brown (2023) examined A and concluded B.

### Example: Synthesis (good)
> Multiple studies have demonstrated X in populations ranging from P1 to P2 (Smith, 2021; Jones, 2022; Brown, 2023). However, the strength of this relationship varies substantially depending on methodological approach: studies using Method1 consistently find larger effects (Smith, 2021; Brown, 2023) than those using Method2 (Jones, 2022), suggesting that choice of measurement instrument may partly explain conflicting findings in the literature.

## The Synthesis Matrix

The most practical tool for organizing a literature synthesis.

### How to build it

Create a table with **sources as rows** and **themes/concepts as columns**:

| Source | Theme A | Theme B | Theme C | Methods | Key Limitations |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Smith (2021) | Finds X supports A | No data on B | Mentions C as future work | RCT, n=500 | Single-site |
| Jones (2022) | Finds X weakly supports A | B is significant factor | — | Survey, n=2000 | Self-report bias |
| Brown (2023) | Contradicts: X does not support A | B confirmed | C explored qualitatively | Mixed methods, n=50 | Small sample |

### What the matrix reveals

Read **columns** (not rows) to see patterns across the literature:
- **Convergence**: Multiple sources agree on a finding → strong evidence base
- **Contradiction**: Sources disagree → explore why (different methods, populations, contexts, definitions?)
- **Gaps**: Empty cells → areas needing further research
- **Methodological patterns**: Certain findings cluster with certain methods → method may influence results

## Organization Strategies

### Thematic (most common, usually best)
Organize around topics, debates, or concepts rather than individual sources. Each section addresses a theme and draws on multiple sources.

**When to use**: Most literature reviews. Especially when the goal is to show the state of knowledge on specific questions.

**Structure example**:
1. Theme A: What do we know about X?
2. Theme B: The role of Y
3. Theme C: Methodological debates
4. Theme D: Emerging directions

### Chronological
Follow the development of ideas over time.

**When to use**: Only when there is a clear developmental progression where later work builds directly on earlier work, and this progression is central to the argument.

**Caution**: Chronological organization often degrades into source-by-source summary. Use sparingly and only when temporal development is the point.

### Methodological
Group studies by their research approach.

**When to use**: When methodological choices significantly affect findings, or when the review's purpose is to evaluate methods.

**Structure example**:
1. Quantitative approaches to measuring X
2. Qualitative investigations of X
3. Mixed methods designs
4. Computational/ML approaches

### Hybrid (often best for complex reviews)
Combine approaches. For example:
- Primarily thematic, with chronological discussion within each theme
- Start with a brief chronological overview, then shift to thematic analysis
- Thematic organization with a separate methodological section

## Synthesis Techniques by Review Type

### For Narrative/Traditional Reviews
1. Identify 3-5 major themes from your reading
2. For each theme, group all relevant sources
3. Write each section by weaving sources into an analytical argument
4. Use transition paragraphs to connect themes

### For Scoping Reviews
1. Map the breadth of literature: How much? What types? What disciplines?
2. Characterize the evidence: methods used, populations studied, geographic coverage
3. Identify clusters and gaps
4. Present findings descriptively with charts/tables showing the landscape

### For Systematic Reviews
1. Apply formal quality assessment to each included study
2. Tabulate study characteristics and findings
3. Assess heterogeneity across studies
4. Synthesize narratively and, if appropriate, quantitatively (meta-analysis)
5. Assess risk of bias across the body of evidence
6. Rate certainty of evidence (e.g., GRADE framework)

## Identifying and Discussing Gaps

Gaps are among the most valuable outputs of a literature review. Look for:

| Gap Type | How to Identify |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Population gaps** | Who has been studied? Who hasn't? (demographics, geographies, contexts) |
| **Methodological gaps** | What methods dominate? What approaches haven't been tried? |
| **Temporal gaps** | Are findings outdated? Has the context changed since key studies were conducted? |
| **Conceptual gaps** | What theoretical connections haven't been explored? What assumptions remain untested? |
| **Outcome gaps** | What outcomes have been measured? What relevant outcomes haven't? |
| **Scale gaps** | What levels of analysis are missing? (individual, community, national, comparative) |

## Handling Contradictions

When sources disagree, do NOT simply list both views and move on. Investigate:

1. **Methodological differences**: Different study designs, measures, or analytical approaches?
2. **Population differences**: Different samples, geographies, or time periods?
3. **Definitional differences**: Are authors measuring the same construct differently?
4. **Context differences**: Different policy environments, historical moments, or institutional settings?
5. **Quality differences**: Is one study methodologically stronger than the other?

Discuss possible explanations for the contradiction. This is often the most analytically valuable part of a literature review.

## Writing the Synthesis

### Paragraph structure for synthesis
Each body paragraph should typically:
1. **Open with an analytical claim** (your voice, your argument)
2. **Support with evidence** from multiple sources
3. **Discuss nuances, caveats, or contradictions**
4. **Connect to the broader argument** or transition to the next point

### Attribution and voice
- Use **author-prominent citations** when the author's identity matters: "Smith (2023), whose pioneering work on MSE established the field, argues that..."
- Use **information-prominent citations** when the finding matters more than the author: "Recent evidence suggests X (Smith, 2023; Jones, 2024)"
- Maintain **your analytical voice** as the driver: "The evidence strongly suggests... however... which raises the question..."

### Connecting sections
Use explicit transitions that show how themes relate:
- "While the previous section established X, the question of Y remains contested..."
- "These methodological debates have practical implications for..."
- "Building on the findings discussed above, a separate line of research has examined..."

## Concept Mapping (for complex reviews)

When the relationships between topics are complex, create a concept map before writing:

1. List all major concepts, theories, and findings on separate nodes
2. Draw connections between related nodes
3. Label each connection with the nature of the relationship (supports, contradicts, extends, applies, measures)
4. Identify clusters (future sections) and bridges (transition points)
5. Look for isolated nodes (potential gaps) and highly connected nodes (central concepts)

This is especially useful for interdisciplinary reviews where connections between fields may not be obvious.
