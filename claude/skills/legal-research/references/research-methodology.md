# Legal Research Methodology

## The Legal Research Process

### Step 1: Preliminary Analysis
Before touching any database:
- Identify the **facts** (who, what, when, where, how)
- Identify the **legal issues** those facts raise
- Identify the **jurisdiction** (which court system, which level)
- Identify the **type of law** (constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law)

### Step 2: Start with Secondary Sources
Begin with secondary sources rather than jumping into case law. This saves significant time by providing context and pointing to key primary authorities.

| Source Type | Best For | Examples |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| Legal encyclopedias | Broad overview of unfamiliar topic | Am. Jur. 2d, Cal. Jur. 3d |
| Treatises | Complex law that varies by jurisdiction | Witkin's CA Criminal Law, LaFave's Search and Seizure |
| Law review articles | In-depth analysis of new/emerging issues | SSRN, HeinOnline |
| Practice guides | Practical how-to guidance | Rutter Group, CEB |
| ALR annotations | 50-state survey on a narrow issue | American Law Reports |
| Restatements | Authoritative synthesis of common law | Restatement (Third) of Torts |

### Step 3: Move to Primary Sources
With a framework from secondary sources, research the controlling statutes, regulations, and case law.

### Step 4: Update and Verify
Use citators (Shepard's, KeyCite, or free alternatives) to confirm every authority is still good law.

### Step 5: Analyze and Synthesize
Organize findings into coherent analysis applying the law to the facts.

## Finding Cases Interpreting a Statute

**Method 1: Notes of Decisions / Citing References**
- Search for the statute section number as a search term in case law databases
- On Westlaw/Lexis: use "Notes of Decisions" (curated cases interpreting each section)
- On Google Scholar: search for the statute citation within case law
- On CourtListener: search for the statute number

**Method 2: Citation Chaining**
- Find one good case interpreting the statute
- Backward chain: read its citations to find the cases it relies on
- Forward chain: find cases that cite it (Google Scholar "Cited by", CourtListener)

**Method 3: Secondary Sources**
- Treatises and practice guides will identify the leading cases
- Annotated codes include case notes organized by topic

**Method 4: Key Number System** (Westlaw only)
- Find one relevant case → identify relevant headnote → click the topic/key number → see all cases classified under that point

## The "One Good Case" Method

The most efficient approach when you have a starting point:

1. **Start with a known relevant case**
2. **Look backward**: What cases does it cite? (its reference list)
3. **Look forward**: What cases cite it? (via citators or "Cited by")
4. **Look laterally**: What cases are classified under the same headnote/topic?
5. **Repeat** from the best new cases found in each step

## Verifying a Case Is Good Law

**Non-negotiable**: Every case relied on must be verified.

### Citator signals

| Signal | Shepard's (Lexis) | KeyCite (Westlaw) | Meaning |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Negative | Red stop sign | Red flag | Reversed, overruled, or superseded — DO NOT RELY |
| Caution | Yellow triangle | Yellow flag | Distinguished, criticized, or limited — proceed with caution |
| Positive | Green diamond | Green C | Cited with approval, followed |
| Neutral | Blue A | — | Cited without significant analysis |

### Free alternatives
- Google Scholar "How cited" feature (limited but useful)
- CourtListener citation data
- Manual checking: search for the case name in more recent decisions

### What to check
1. Has the case been explicitly overruled?
2. Has it been distinguished on facts similar to yours?
3. Has the statute been amended to override the court's interpretation?
4. Has a higher court granted review?
5. Is there a more recent case from the same court that modifies the analysis?

## Case Briefing: IRAC Method

For each case you analyze in depth:

1. **Facts**: Legally relevant facts only, as the court found them
2. **Issue**: The precise legal question, framed as a question
3. **Rule**: The legal standard the court applied (abstract, without party names)
4. **Application**: How the court applied the rule to the specific facts — which facts were dispositive and why
5. **Conclusion/Holding**: What the court decided and the disposition (affirmed, reversed, remanded)

**Also note**:
- **Procedural posture**: How the case arrived at this court
- **Concurrences**: May signal future doctrinal shifts
- **Dissents**: May become the basis for future overruling
- **Dicta vs. holding**: Only the holding is binding. Test: "If I remove this statement, does the outcome change?"

## Legal Memorandum Structure

For presenting research findings in a formal legal analysis:

**Question Presented**: One sentence beginning with "Whether..." or "Under [law], does/is..." Incorporates the legal standard and key facts.

**Brief Answer**: 1-3 sentences. Direct answer (Yes/No/Probably), then core reasoning.

**Statement of Facts**: All facts referenced in the Discussion, presented objectively, including unfavorable facts.

**Discussion**: Organized using CREAC:
- **C**onclusion (thesis sentence)
- **R**ule (synthesized from cases and statutes)
- **E**xplanation (case illustrations showing how courts applied the rule)
- **A**pplication (applying the rule to our facts)
- **C**onclusion (restating the prediction)

For multi-issue memos: roadmap paragraph first, then separate CREAC section for each issue.

**Conclusion**: Summary of predictions on each issue, without citations. Note level of certainty.

## Evaluating Precedential Value

Factors that increase a case's weight:

| Factor | Stronger | Weaker |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Court level | Supreme Court | Trial court |
| Jurisdiction | Binding authority | Persuasive only |
| Recency | Recent | Old (unless seminal) |
| Factual similarity | Close match | Distinguishable |
| Decision | Unanimous | Split / plurality |
| Treatment | Widely cited approvingly | Rarely cited or criticized |
| Publication | Published | Unpublished |

## Handling Conflicting Authority

| Conflict Type | Resolution |
|---------------|------------|
| Same jurisdiction, different levels | Higher court controls |
| Same level, same jurisdiction (e.g., two CA appellate districts) | Choose based on recency, reasoning, alignment with Supreme Court direction |
| Different jurisdictions | Neither binds. Evaluate reasoning, factual similarity, trend in the law |

**Always**: Acknowledge conflicting authority. Address contrary binding authority directly. Failing to address known contrary authority is an ethical issue.
