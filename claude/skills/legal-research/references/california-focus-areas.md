# California Focus Areas

Specialized knowledge for the legal issues we most commonly research.

## 1. Racial Justice Act (Penal Code 745)

### Overview
The California Racial Justice Act (AB 2542, effective 2021; amended by AB 256, effective 2023) prohibits the state from seeking or obtaining a conviction, or imposing a sentence, on the basis of race, ethnicity, or national origin.

### The Four Violation Pathways (Pen. Code § 745(a))

1. **Bias/animus by officials** (§ 745(a)(1)): A judge, attorney, law enforcement officer, or expert witness exhibited bias or animus toward the defendant because of race
2. **Racially discriminatory language** (§ 745(a)(2)): During trial, in court, or during plea negotiations, racially discriminatory language about the defendant's race was used, whether or not intentional
3. **Racially disparate charging** (§ 745(a)(3)): The defendant was charged or convicted of a more serious offense than defendants of other races who committed similar offenses and were similarly situated, and the prosecution cannot establish that the disparity was because of a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason
4. **Racially disparate sentencing** (§ 745(a)(4)): A longer or more severe sentence was imposed than on similarly situated defendants of other races, and the prosecution cannot establish a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason

### Evidentiary Standards
- Statistical evidence is admissible and may be sufficient standing alone to establish a violation under (a)(3) and (a)(4)
- Aggregate data may be used; the defendant need not identify specific comparator cases
- The defendant may rely on statistical evidence from the county, the charging agency, or the state
- Evidence of implicit bias is relevant

### RJA Discovery
- Available only in a pending proceeding where the defendant has alleged a § 745(a) violation
- Not a freestanding discovery right — must be connected to a pending motion
- Can request non-privileged prosecution materials
- Statistical data from prosecuting agencies is a key tool

### Retroactivity (AB 256)
- **Jan 1, 2023**: Retroactive to cases not yet final on appeal
- **Jan 1, 2024**: Extends to people currently serving sentences for felonies
- **Jan 1, 2025**: Extends to all felony convictions entered after 2015 regardless of custody status
- **Jan 1, 2026**: Every felony conviction and juvenile commitment becomes eligible

## 2. Police Transparency Laws

### SB 1421 (eff. Jan 1, 2019)
Makes public records of:
- Officer-involved shootings and use of force resulting in death or great bodily injury
- Sustained findings of sexual assault by officers
- Sustained findings of dishonesty in investigations or court testimony

### SB 16 (eff. Jan 1, 2022)
Expands public records to include:
- Sustained findings of excessive force
- Sustained findings of unlawful searches/arrests
- Sustained findings of discriminatory conduct or failure to intervene
- Sustained findings involving illegal conduct relating to dishonesty

### SB 2 — Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act (eff. Jan 1, 2022)
- Creates statewide decertification system administered by POST
- Grounds: dishonesty, abuse of power, physical abuse, sexual assault, bias, serious misconduct
- Peace Officer Standards Accountability Division investigates and recommends decertification
- Records retained for 30 years
- Complaints can trigger decertification hearings

### POBR — Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights (Gov. Code 3301-3313)
- Governs internal affairs investigations of peace officers
- Procedural protections: right to representation, notification requirements, interrogation limits
- One-year statute of limitations on discipline (Gov. Code § 3304(d)) — key litigation issue
- Has been used to shield misconduct records from disclosure
- **Tension with transparency laws**: Courts have increasingly ruled that SB 1421/SB 16 override POBR confidentiality for covered records

## 3. Discovery in Criminal Cases

### Brady v. Maryland (1963)
- Constitutional duty (Due Process, 14th Amendment) to disclose material exculpatory evidence
- "Material" = could reasonably affect the trial outcome
- Covers both exculpatory and impeachment evidence
- Duty extends to evidence known to police, not just prosecutors
- Violation requires: (1) favorable evidence, (2) suppressed by prosecution, (3) prejudice (materiality)

### Pitchess Motions (Evid. Code 1043-1047; Pen. Code 832.7-832.8)
- Named after *Pitchess v. Superior Court* (1974) 11 Cal.3d 531
- Allows defense access to officer personnel file complaints and investigation records
- **Procedure**:
  1. File motion describing records sought and showing good cause
  2. Court conducts in camera review of personnel file
  3. Court discloses relevant, non-privileged information
- 10 court days notice required
- Good cause = specific factual scenario showing officer misconduct plausibly occurred
- **Brady intersection**: Personnel file info may be Brady material; if it meets Brady materiality, it generally satisfies Pitchess good cause

### RJA Discovery
- Available only in a pending proceeding with an alleged § 745(a) violation
- Not freestanding — must be tied to a pending motion
- Can request non-privileged prosecution materials
- Statistical data from prosecution agencies is the key tool
- Can include charging data, plea offer data, enhancement filing data, sentencing data

### Other Discovery Provisions
- **Pen. Code § 1054-1054.7**: California's reciprocal discovery statute. Prosecution must disclose witness names/addresses, statements, real evidence, felony convictions of material witnesses, exculpatory evidence
- **Pen. Code § 1102**: Defense right to inspect prosecution evidence
- **Cal. Rules of Court 4.111**: Informal discovery in misdemeanors

### Research Strategy
1. Search for cases interpreting the specific discovery provision at issue
2. For Pitchess: search "Evidence Code section 1043" or "Pitchess" in CA appellate opinions
3. For Brady: search "Brady v. Maryland" with specific factual terms
4. For RJA discovery: search "Penal Code 745" AND "discovery"
5. Check for recent amendments to discovery statutes
6. Review secondary sources: Witkin's California Criminal Law (Discovery chapter)

## 4. Standing and Injunctive Relief in Civil Rights Cases

### The Lyons Framework
*City of Los Angeles v. Lyons* (1983) 461 U.S. 95: Standing to seek damages for past harm does NOT automatically confer standing for injunctive relief against future harm.

**To obtain injunctive relief, must show**:
- A "real and immediate threat" of being subjected to the challenged conduct again
- Past exposure alone is insufficient
- Creates a high barrier for individual plaintiffs challenging systemic practices

### Strategies to Overcome Lyons
1. **Demonstrate an ongoing policy or practice** (not just a single incident)
2. **Show personal exposure**: Plaintiff lives in the jurisdiction, has regular contact with the agency
3. **Class certification**: The class as a whole may satisfy the "real and immediate threat" requirement even if individual plaintiffs face difficulty
4. **Organizational standing**: An organization whose mission is frustrated by the challenged conduct
5. **Municipal liability** under *Monell v. Department of Social Services*: Challenge the policy itself rather than individual conduct

### Key Statutes
- **42 U.S.C. § 1983**: Civil rights claims against state actors
- **42 U.S.C. § 1988**: Attorney's fees in civil rights cases
- Available remedies: compensatory damages, punitive damages, declaratory relief, injunctive relief

### Research Strategy
1. Search for Lyons and its progeny in the specific context (police practices, surveillance, etc.)
2. Look for successful injunctive relief cases with similar facts
3. Research class certification standards for civil rights claims
4. Check the Civil Rights Litigation Clearinghouse (clearinghouse.net) for consent decrees and reform cases

## 5. Fourth Amendment and Surveillance Law

### Core Framework
- **Katz v. United States** (1967): A "search" occurs when government violates a "reasonable expectation of privacy"
- Two-part test: (1) subjective expectation of privacy, (2) society recognizes it as reasonable
- **Carpenter v. United States** (2018): Warrant required for historical cell-site location information (CSLI)
- **Riley v. California** (2014): Warrant required to search cell phones incident to arrest

### California Electronic Communications Privacy Act (CalECPA)
- **Pen. Code §§ 1546-1546.4** (SB 178, eff. Jan 1, 2016)
- Requires warrant for electronic communications content, metadata, and device searches
- Broader protections than federal law in many respects
- Covers: email, text messages, location data, cloud storage, device searches
- Exceptions: emergency, consent, device search incident to arrest (but see Riley)

### Emerging Surveillance Technologies
Research areas where the law is actively developing:
- **Automated License Plate Readers (ALPRs)**: SB 34 (2015) regulates data sharing. EFF maintains CA ALPR policy database.
- **Geofence warrants**: Requests for location data on all devices in a geographic area during a time window
- **Keyword warrants**: Requests for information on all users who searched specific terms
- **Facial recognition**: Several CA cities have banned government use (San Francisco, Oakland, Berkeley)
- **Stingray/cell-site simulators**: SB 741 mandates use policies
- **Predictive policing algorithms**: Legal challenges emerging
- **Drone surveillance**: Growing body of case law

### Community Control Over Police Surveillance (CCOPS)
Oakland was the first city to adopt a CCOPS framework requiring city council approval for new surveillance technology. Several other CA cities have followed.
